Climate Adaptation & Maternal Health: A Cost-Effectiveness Analysis 

<span class="tag">Climate &amp; Health</span>
<span class="tag">Cost-Effectiveness Analysis</span>
<span class="tag">Maternal Health</span>
<span class="tag">R</span>
<span class="tag">Global Health</span>

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)—excessive bleeding after childbirth—is a leading cause of maternal death in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). Rising global temperatures make this worse: heat degrades the standard drug used to prevent PPH (oxytocin), reducing its effectiveness in hot climates. This project builds a cost-effectiveness model comparing standard oxytocin against heat-stable carbetocin, a newer alternative that remains effective without refrigeration.

Using climate projections from CMIP6 and population data across 2020–2059, the model estimates how many maternal deaths could be prevented and at what cost across different world regions, providing decision-makers with evidence to guide maternal health procurement in a warming world.

Pulmonary Embolism Incidence Patterns: PE-EHR+ Study 

<span class="tag">Health Informatics</span>
<span class="tag">Electronic Health Records</span>
<span class="tag">Python</span>
<span class="tag">Epidemiology</span>

Pulmonary embolism (PE)—a blood clot in the lungs—is a life-threatening but often preventable condition. Despite its severity, patterns of PE diagnosis and care complexity are not well-characterized across diverse patient populations. This project uses electronic health record (EHR) data from the PE-EHR+ dataset to analyze how PE is diagnosed, managed, and documented across hospitals and patient groups.

Working in Python, the analysis examines trends in PE incidence, differences across demographic groups, and the complexity of clinical care pathways—surfacing insights that can improve hospital protocols and reduce preventable mortality.

SARS-CoV-2 Variant Dominance Analysis (2022–2024) 

<span class="tag">Epidemiology</span>
<span class="tag">Infectious Disease</span>
<span class="tag">R</span>
<span class="tag">Data Visualization</span>
<span class="tag">Reproducible Research</span>

As the COVID-19 pandemic evolved, successive waves were driven by different SARS-CoV-2 variants—each with distinct transmission rates, immune escape potential, and clinical severity. Understanding which variants dominated, and when, is essential for evaluating public health responses and preparing for future outbreaks.

This project analyzes U.S. surveillance data from December 2022 through October 2024, tracking the emergence and dominance of variants that achieved greater than 5% national prevalence across successive epidemic waves. Using R with fully reproducible, version-controlled code, the analysis produces clear visualizations of variant succession to inform vaccine strategy and outbreak preparedness planning.
